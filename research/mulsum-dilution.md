# Was mulsum/conditum drunk straight or diluted with water?

*Created 2026-07-18 10:35.*

Scope: whether Roman honeyed wine (*mulsum*) and spiced honeyed wine (*conditum*, e.g. Apicius 1.1 *conditum paradoxum*), once prepared, were drunk essentially as-made or further cut with water at the table the way ordinary *merum* was routinely diluted at the *krater*/symposium. Sourced testimony, not inference.

## Summary verdict

**No ancient source says mulsum or conditum was further diluted with water at the table. Confidence: probable that they were served essentially as-prepared (not routinely cut with table water like ordinary wine) — but this rests on the shape of the recipe/serving evidence, not on any explicit statement either way. The direct evidence is genuinely thin; this is under-documented in the sources.**

The strongest positive evidence is what the recipes themselves show:

1. **These recipes describe a *finished* drink, and where any "cut to taste" step is named, the diluent is wine, not water.** Apicius' traveller's spiced honey (*conditum melizomum viatorium*, 1.2) is an explicit concentrate — but the instruction at serving is to mix it "with spiced wine", never with water. Ordinary table wine, by contrast, was cut with water. So the one place a Roman recipe tells you how to adjust strength of a honeyed drink at the moment of serving, it adjusts with wine.
2. **Conditum paradoxum (Apicius 1.1) already contains its wine.** The recipe is a boiled honey-and-spice base into which you finally pour 18 *sextarii* of light wine (against 6 *sextarii* honey + ~2 used in the boil). The output is already a wine-strength beverage — a finished conditum, not a syrup awaiting water.
3. **Mulsum is described as a fixed-ratio finished aperitif** (Pliny ~5:1 must:honey; Columella; the common ~4:1 wine:honey), served at the *gustatio*. No source appends a "then add water" step.

The widespread blog/popular claim that "mulsum was diluted with water like all Roman wine" is an **extrapolation from the general dilution rule, not a cited fact** — I found no primary source applying the water-dilution custom to mulsum or conditum specifically. Modern reconstructors do dilute conditum paradoxum, but explicitly because it is cloyingly sweet to a modern palate, which is a reconstruction judgment, not ancient testimony.

Net: Damon's working hypothesis (specialty gustatio drinks served closer to as-prepared, because further watering undercuts the point) is consistent with all the evidence and contradicted by none — but it should be stated as the better-supported reading, not as settled fact. Conditum specifically has a firmer footing than mulsum, because the viatorium recipe directly shows the diluent-of-choice was wine.

## Citation-by-citation evidence

### Apicius, *De Re Coquinaria* 1.1 — Conditum Paradoxum (strongest single item)

Verbatim (Vehling translation via LacusCurtius):

> "Into a copper bowl put 6 sextarii of honey and 2 sextarii of wine; heat on a slow fire... At the boiling point add a dash of cold wine, retire from stove and skim. Repeat this twice or three times, let it rest till the next day, and skim again. Then add 4 ounces of crushed pepper, 3 scruples of mastich, a drachm each of nard or laurel leaves and saffron, 5 drachms of roasted date stones crushed and previously soaked in wine... **When this is properly done add 18 sextarii of light wine.** To clarify it perfectly, add crushed charcoal... and carefully strain or filter through the charcoal."

Reading: the recipe *ends* by folding in 18 sextarii of wine and clarifying. The deliverable is a ready-to-drink wine-based conditum, not a concentrate. No water step. The final honey:total-wine ratio is roughly 6 : ~20 sextarii, i.e. the wine dominates the finished drink.

### Apicius, *De Re Coquinaria* 1.2 — Conditum Melizomum Viatorium (traveller's spiced honey)

Verbatim (Vehling via LacusCurtius):

> "The wayfarer's honey refresher... is made in this manner: flavor honey with ground pepper and skim. **In the moment of serving put honey in a cup, as much as is desired to obtain the right degree of sweetness, and mix with spiced wine** not more than a needed quantity; also add some wine to the spiced honey to facilitate its flow and the mixing."

Reading: this is the linchpin for *conditum* as a category. It is explicitly a portable honey **concentrate** dissolved at the point of use — and the diluent named twice is **wine (spiced wine)**, not water. This is direct evidence that when a Roman honeyed-wine product was adjusted to taste at serving, wine was the medium. It undercuts the assumption that these products slotted into the ordinary "cut with water" regime.

### Pliny the Elder, *Natural History* 14

- **14.85 (honey-wine / mulsum composition):** "it is made from must, in the proportion of thirty pints of must of a dry quality to six pints of honey and a cup of salt, this mixture being brought just to the boil; this produces a dry-flavoured liquor." → mulsum given as a finished ~5:1 preparation; no water-at-table step.
- **14.149 (general dilution custom):** of foreign grain liquors, people "quaff liquors of this kind neat and do not temper their strength by diluting them, **as is done with wine**." → confirms ordinary *wine* was normally watered, but says nothing about mulsum/conditum; it is the general rule the blogs over-extend.
- **14.138:** "to enable us to take more, we reduce its strength by means of a linen strainer" — about straining/weakening wine, again general, not mulsum-specific.

Net from Pliny: he documents the general watering of table wine and gives mulsum as a fixed finished recipe, but never says mulsum was itself watered.

### Columella, *De Re Rustica* 12.41 — mulsum

Columella's mulsum is made by combining honey with must (commonly rendered ~1 part honey to ~4 parts must, i.e. adding ~10 lbs honey to a jar of must), sealed ~30 days, then decanted and smoked/aged. This is a **stored, finished product** analogous to a wine — prepared once at wine-strength, not a syrup. No serving-time water instruction. (Note: I worked from the standard summaries of 12.41 plus the Latin at latin.it; I did not verify a full English 12.41 line-by-line, so treat the ratio as the conventional figure rather than a checked quote.)

### Cato, *De Agricultura* 107-109 — NOT a mulsum recipe (correction)

Worth flagging: **Cato 107-109 is not about mulsum.** 107 is about coating wine-jar rims with a boiled must/iris/melilot paste; 108 is a test for whether wine will keep (boiling it with barley); the surrounding chapters (104-125) are Greek wine, sea-water treatment, Coan wine, hellebore/medicinal wines, myrtle wine. Cato gives **no dedicated mulsum recipe** in this run, and nothing here bears on serving dilution. If a Cato "mulsum" citation is wanted it does not live at 107-109.

### Athenaeus, *Deipnosophistae* — general mixing custom (context, not mulsum)

Athenaeus is the main store of Greek wine-mixing lore: mixing ratios debated across authorities (e.g. 10.426b-427a, 10.430d-431b; Hesiod's 3:1 water:wine, Alcaeus' stronger 1:2), the *krater* named from "to mix", *akratos* = unmixed = considered barbaric / medicinal-only. This establishes the **ordinary-wine** dilution regime that mulsum/conditum are being compared against — but it is about table wine at the symposium, not about honeyed aperitifs. (I did not find a passage in which Athenaeus says honeyed/spiced wine was itself watered.)

### Martial — mulsum at the gustatio (placement, not dilution)

Martial repeatedly puts mulsum in the *gustatio* alongside eggs and other starters (the appetizer course; cf. the "ab ovo" association of eggs with the first course). This corroborates mulsum as a discrete **pre-dinner aperitif** — a different serving context from the main-meal/comissatio wine that was watered at the krater — but Martial does not describe watering the mulsum itself.

### Smith's *Dictionary* / Horace, *Satires* 2.4.24 — "leni mulso"

The scholarly "Coena" article (Smith's) cites Horace recommending mild mulsum: *"Leni praecordia mulso prolueris melius"* — i.e. flushing the stomach with a **mild/smooth** (*leni*) mulsum. This shows mulsum came in stronger and gentler versions, but "leni" describes how the mulsum was *made* (less honey / milder), not a table-side addition of water. Suggestive of a strength spectrum, not evidence of water-cutting.

## Confidence and open questions

- **Conditum:** *probable* it was served as a finished wine-strength drink, with any sweetness adjustment made using wine (viatorium 1.2 is near-direct on this). The "syrup you dilute with water" framing common online is imported from modern reconstruction, and where an ancient recipe does describe the concentrate-and-dilute method it dilutes with wine.
- **Mulsum:** *probable but thinner*. It is consistently a fixed-ratio finished aperitif; no source waters it. But the absence is an argument from silence, and the general Roman aversion to *merum* means one cannot fully exclude some watering at table. Honest statement: no surviving source settles it.
- **What would settle it:** a passage explicitly describing pouring/watering mulsum at serving, or a scholiast/agronomist saying mulsum was drunk *merum*. I did not find one. Grocock & Grainger's 2006 critical Apicius commentary is the place most likely to adjudicate the conditum-as-concentrate question directly; I could not access its text online, only downstream popular echoes of the "concentrate/syrup" idea. Worth a library check if Damon wants to firm this up.
