The ancient Mediterranean was a large and varied place, and its cooking changed enormously across time and geography. The cuisine of fourth-century-BC Athens was surely very different from that of sixth-century-AD Gaul. But there was continuity underneath the variety, much as Hungarian, French, and Norwegian cooking are distinct and yet recognizably part of one broad European tradition. Like the modern Mediterranean, the ancient Mediterranean leaned on olive oil, wine, wheat, and bread, with lentils and other legumes doing much of the work of protein. There was a fondness, especially in luxury cooking, for fish and other seafood, and the wealthy ate all manner of meat and fowl—including some things, like flamingos and dormice, that read as stranger to us than they would have to a Roman.

For all that overlaps with modern Mediterranean food, the flavor profile was quite different. In Roman cooking especially, fish sauce was everywhere. *Garum* and its relatives are essentially the same product as the fish sauce of modern Southeast Asia: fish, heavily salted along with their guts, are left to break down. The salt keeps out the bacteria you don't want, while the enzymes in the guts autolyze the flesh—the fish digest themselves—releasing glutamic acid and producing a sauce that is intensely salty and umami but not really fishy at all. It functions the way soy sauce does (soy sauce itself descends from early fish sauces), or bouillon, or parmesan. I've cooked with it for people who dislike seafood without telling them, and they've never noticed.

The other distinctive note is the herbs and seeds. Roman cooking is sometimes described as heavily spiced, which I think is mostly a misunderstanding. What *Apicius* actually uses in quantity are herbs and seeds from the carrot family—cumin, coriander, caraway, and lovage (which tastes much like celery)—along with things like parsley, thyme, oregano, and mint. These were local to the Mediterranean, not costly imports. The real question is why modern Europeans use them so little, not why the Romans used them so much. Eastern spices are the exception rather than the rule. Pepper arrived around the first century BC and was taken up with startling speed: it turns up in nearly every recipe in *Apicius*, which often close with the instruction to grind pepper over the dish before serving—desserts included. Beyond the occasional cinnamon leaf or dried ginger, the other eastern spices barely appear, or appear only in late recipes; during the imperial era they seem to have belonged more to perfumes and medicines than to the kitchen.

The result is at once familiar and strange. The base is clearly Mediterranean, but the seeds can recall an Indian curry, the fish sauce lends a Southeast Asian edge, and the taste for sweet-and-sour sauces is a little reminiscent of Chinese cooking—though with a more complicated balance of herbs and spices than any of those comparisons quite captures.

A note on how this section is organized. Rather than a single catalogue of dishes, the recipes here are grouped by source, because the surviving record is patchy and each source shapes what we can see. The great majority of recipes come from *Apicius*, a collection of around 450 recipes compiled probably in the late fourth century, though it draws on earlier material written down and reworked across the first through fourth centuries; it gives a reasonable picture of elite Roman cooking under the empire, luxurious dishes and simple vegetable preparations alike. The shorter *Vinidarius*, some thirty recipes from around 500, is the only other surviving recipe collection in the same tradition. Everything else survives in fragments and quotation—the *Deipnosophistae* of Athenaeus preserving lost Greek works like Archestratus' poem on luxury dining, the medical writers Galen and Anthimus recording plainer everyday food, the agricultural manuals of Cato and Columella tucking in a handful of recipes for cakes and preserves. Each source has its own page; the essay above is just an orientation to the whole.
