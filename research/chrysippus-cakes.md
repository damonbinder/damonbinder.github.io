# Chrysippus of Tyana's fourteen cakes — dating and cross-reference against Apicius

*Created 2026-07-11 14:11.*

Scope: (1) what scholarship establishes about Chrysippus of Tyana's date; (2) how many of his fourteen named cakes have a same-brand twin in the Latin Apicius. Research only — no site/recipe files touched.

## TL;DR

- **Locus.** The cake list is [Athenaeus, *Deipnosophistae* 14.647c](https://www.attalus.org/old/athenaeus14c.html) (Book 14, ch. 57, Casaubon pp. 647–648), introduced: "Chrysippus of Tyana, in his book called *The Art of Making Bread* [Ἀρτοκοπικός], enumerates the following species and genera of cheesecakes…"
- **Date.** No firm date is fixed by primary evidence. Reference works file him as [RE "Chrysippos 21"](https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q11916102); secondary food-history sources conventionally call him **1st century AD** ([Wikipedia, *History of bread*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_bread)), but the only hard bracket from the text is a *terminus ante quem* of Athenaeus himself (*fl.* c. 200 AD), who says Chrysippus "seems to have been little known" before him ([Smith's *Dictionary*, via hellenicaworld](https://www.hellenicaworld.com/Greece/Person/en/ChrysippusOfTyana.html)).
- **Strongest datable anchor = the Apician cake.** The *apikianon* is named after the Tiberian gourmet M. Gavius Apicius (*fl.* c. 14–37 AD), so the list — at least in the form Athenaeus quotes — cannot predate the mid-1st century AD. That is a solid *terminus post quem*; the internal-evidence argument (Latin gentilic names ⇒ imperial-era author) is sound as a floor but does not by itself narrow the date further.
- **Apicius tally (of 14): 3 solid twins, 0 additional plausible, 11 none.** Solid = **Apician, Julian, Terentine**. Everything else (Krassianon, Toutianon, Sabellikon, Kanopika, Perloukidon, Kappadokikon, Hedybia, Marupton, Plikion, Gouttaton, Mondianon) has **no** same-name dish in Apicius. Note the near-miss: *Gouttaton* shares a root with Apicius's *olei guttas* ("drops of oil"), but that is an ingredient phrase, not a branded dish — not a twin.
- **Headline surprise.** The overlap is exactly the three we already knew and no more. The eleven un-matched names split into (a) plausibly Latin brands that simply don't survive in our Apicius (*Krassianon*, *Toutianon*, *Sabellikon*, *Mondianon*), and (b) names that look Greek/toponymic rather than Roman-gentilic (*Kanopika* = Canopus in Egypt; *Kappadokikon* = Cappadocia; *Hedybia* from Greek *hēdys* "sweet"). So the "imperial branded-dish vocabulary" hypothesis is only partly borne out — the shared layer is real but thin.

## Q1 — Chrysippus of Tyana's date

**Who he is.** Chrysippus of Tyana (in Cappadocia) wrote the Ἀρτοκοπικός ("On Bread-Making"), a treatise on breads, cakes, and sweetmeats, known **only** through Athenaeus, who calls him a σοφὸς πεμματολόγος ("learned cake-expert"). He is catalogued in Pauly-Wissowa as **Chrysippos (21)** ([Wikidata Q11916102](https://www.wikidata.org/wiki/Q11916102)).

**(a) Conventional date.** There is no dating tradition anchored in external testimony. The standard 19th-c. reference treatment (Smith's *Dictionary of Greek and Roman Biography*, reproduced at [hellenicaworld](https://www.hellenicaworld.com/Greece/Person/en/ChrysippusOfTyana.html)) says only that he "seems to have been little known before the time of" Athenaeus — i.e. it declines to date him beyond *before c. 200 AD*. Modern food-history summaries assign **1st century AD** ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_bread)), but this is a soft convention, not a sourced argument; ToposText and Perseus give **no floruit** at all ([ToposText](https://topostext.org/people/14522)).

**(b) The internal-evidence (gentilic-names) argument.** I did not find this argument made explicitly and rigorously in the reference literature I could reach; the sources note the Latin/Roman character of the names in passing but do not build a dating case on it. As an inference it is valid but weak on its own: Grecized Latin gentilic/toponymic adjectives (Terentine, Julian, Apician, Crassian, Sabellic) presuppose a Roman cultural horizon and so point to an imperial-era, Latin-aware author — consistent with 1st–2nd c. AD — but the presence of Latin brand-names only forces the author to postdate the naming of the dishes, not to be strictly contemporary with Athenaeus.

**(c) Strongest substantiable *terminus post quem*.** The **Apician** cake is the load-bearing anchor. The gourmet M. Gavius Apicius flourished under Tiberius (c. 14–37 AD) ([Wikipedia, *Marcus Gavius Apicius*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcus_Gavius_Apicius)); dishes styled *Apicianus/-a/-um* are named for him. So the list Athenaeus quotes cannot, as it stands, predate the mid-1st century AD. Combined with the Athenaeus *terminus ante quem*, the defensible bracket is **c. mid-1st century – c. 200 AD**, with "1st century AD" being the common (but under-argued) point estimate. *This bracket is my synthesis from the sourced facts; only the two endpoints (Apicius's floruit; Athenaeus's) are directly sourced.*

**Tally: 3 solid / 0 additional plausible / 11 none.**

**Athenaeus elsewhere / Greek origin.** The list sits inside Athenaeus's long *plakous* (cheesecake) discussion (14.643–648); the general cake-genera and Attic bread-name catalogues nearby (e.g. 14.643e ff., and the bread-name list at 3.109–115) do not, from what I found, re-attest these fourteen brand-names outside the Chrysippus quotation. Several of the fourteen are transparently Greek or Greek-toponymic rather than Latin-derived: **Hedybia** (*hēdys*), **Kanopika** (Canopus), **Kappadokikon** (Cappadocia), and probably **Plikion** and **Marupton**. So the "shared imperial Latin brand vocabulary" is confined to the Latin-gentilic subset (Terentine, Julian, Apician, Crassian, Sabellic, possibly Montian), and only three of those actually recur in Apicius.

## Terentinum etymology note

The live question is *gens Terentia* (a Roman family adjective, "Terentine") vs. *Tarentum/Taras* (the south-Italian city, "Tarentine"). Bearing on it:

- **Apicius spells it with -e-, not -a-**: *Terentinum / Terentina* consistently (4.2.13, 4.3.2, 8.7.1) — never *Tarentinum*. Athenaeus's Greek likewise gives *terentinon*. The vowel favours the **gentilic** (*Terentius* → *Terentinus*) over the toponym (*Tarentum* → *Tarentinus*), which would show -a-.
- This groups *Terentinon* with the other unambiguous Roman-family brands in the list (Julian ← *Iulius*, Apician ← *Apicius*, Crassian ← *Crassus*), which is the more economical reading: a series of dishes named for gentes, not a lone city-adjective mixed in.
- Caveat: -e-/-a- interchange is not decisive in later Latin/Greek transmission, and a Tarentine origin can't be formally excluded on spelling alone. But on the balance of the surrounding names and the consistent orthography, **gens Terentia is the better-supported reading.** *(This is my inference from the orthography and list context, not a claim I found stated in a cited authority.)*
