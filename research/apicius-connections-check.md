## 1. Pliny / nitrum-greening — PARTLY CONFIRMED

Exact Latin (Pliny, *Naturalis Historia* 19.143, on garden vegetables):

> "nitrum in coquendo etiam viriditatem custodit, ut et Apiciana coctura, oleo ac sale, priusquam coquantur, maceratis."

"*Nitrum* in cooking also preserves the green colour, as does the Apician method [of cooking], the [vegetables] being steeped in oil and salt before they are cooked."

- The passage does exist and is about *nitrum* preserving *viriditas* — matching Apicius 3.1 (*ut omne olus smaragdinum fiat ... si cum nitro coquatur*).
- But Pliny writes **Apiciana coctura** — "Apician cooking" as an adjective — not "Apicius devised this." He mentions the Apician method as a parallel technique, and pairs *nitrum* with the oil-and-salt maceration of the Apician style. So "Pliny attributes to Apicius the trick" overstates it slightly: he associates the greening effect with *nitrum* and separately name-drops the Apician cooking method. Safest public phrasing: *"Pliny notes the same nitrum-greening effect and links it to 'Apician cooking' (NH 19.143)."*
- This is **not** a conflation with the famous figs/*ficatum* liver passage (*NH* 8.209) — that's a separate, correctly-distinct Apicius attribution.
- Numbering caveat: Rackham's Loeb gives §143; the Latin-Wikisource text places it at §142 (chapter XLII). Cite as 19.143 with the §142 variant noted, or just "*NH* 19.42".

## 2. Palladius rosatum — CONFIRMED (date correction)

- Palladius, *Opus Agriculturae*, has a *rosatum* recipe under the November work (Book 11.17): rose petals (white heel removed) sewn into a linen bag, steeped in wine ~7 days, petals renewed twice more, then strained and honeyed. Structurally the same steep-petals-in-wine method as Apicius 1.4.
- **Date fix:** "c. 350" is too early. Scholarly consensus places Palladius (Rutilius Taurus Aemilianus Palladius) in the **late 4th to early/mid 5th century AD** — the *Opus Agriculturae* is dated late 4th / early 5th c. Recommend "late 4th / early 5th c. AD" rather than a single "c. 350" figure.

## 3. Athenaeus rose patina — CONFIRMED (correcting the earlier "refuted" verdict)

**The earlier verdict in this file was wrong.** An earlier pass concluded there was "no near-identical counterpart" in Athenaeus and that the dish was "Apicius-only." That is incorrect: the parallel exists and is close to the point of being the same recipe. The earlier pass evidently looked only at the rose-as-garland material in Book 15 and missed the cook's-boast scene in Book 9.

*Corrected 2026-07-11.*

### Locus

- **Athenaeus, *Deipnosophistae* 9.406a–b** (Casaubon page 406). Book **9**. In C.D. Yonge's Bohn/Gutenberg translation this is the "Dish of Roses" at printed pages 639–640 (Yonge's running section 70), matching Damon's pointer ("Gutenberg pg 640, or 9.70").
- Greek text below is the Gulick (Loeb) edition as digitised by Perseus (`Perseus:text:2008.01.0405`), with the Casaubon-406 milestones confirmed in the TEI.

### Attribution — the key finding: NOT a pre-Apician comic quotation

- The Dish of Roses speech is spoken by an **unnamed cook character inside Athenaeus's own dramatic frame**, in dialogue with the diner **Aemilianus**. It is Athenaeus's own narrative prose, delivered in the cook's first person ("ῥοδωνιὰν καλῶ … ἐπέβαλον … ἐνέβαλον").
- It is **not** introduced as a quotation from any comic poet or play. The two comic poets named nearby are decoration the characters cite in the surrounding banter — **Hegesippus**, *Adelphoi* (*Brethren*), and **Demetrius**, *The Areopagite* — but neither is credited with the rose dish; those quotes cover only the "praising cookery" and "what my art has earned me" lines. (Do not conflate: the "Areopagites valued them at 200 drachmas" line at Casaubon ~167, Book 4, is an unrelated passage.)
- **Consequence for dating:** because the dish is Athenaeus's own frame narrative, its real anchor is **Athenaeus himself, c. 200 AD** — *not* an earlier comic source. So this passage does **not** establish that the dish predates Apicius. Athenaeus (fl. c. 200 AD) is roughly contemporary with, or slightly later than, the 1st-c-AD Apician material later compiled into *De re coquinaria* (compiled 4th–5th c AD). The honest statement is: the two are near-contemporary witnesses to the same haute-cuisine dish, with no evidence that either predates or copies the other.

### Greek text (Athenaeus 9.406a–b, Gulick/Perseus)

> ῥοδωνιὰν καλῶ μὲν τὴν λοπάδα ταύτην ἐγώ· ἐσκεύασται δ' οὕτως, ἵνα καὶ ἥδυσμα στεφανωτικὸν μὴ μόνον ἐπὶ τῆς κεφαλῆς λαβὼν σχῇς, ἀλλὰ καὶ ἔνδον σεαυτοῦ καὶ πανδαισίᾳ τὸ σωμάτιον πᾶν ἑστιάσῃς. ῥόδα τὰ εὐοσμότατα ἐν ἴγδει τρίψας ἐπέβαλον ἐγκεφάλους ὀρνίθων τε καὶ χοίρων ἑφθοὺς σφόδρα ἐξινιασθέντας καὶ τῶν ᾠῶν τὰ χρυσᾶ, μεθ' ἃ ἔλαιον, γάρον, πέπερι, οἶνον. καὶ ταῦτα διατρίψας ἐπιμελῶς ἐνέβαλον εἰς λοπάδα καινήν, ἁπαλὸν καὶ συνεχὲς διδοὺς τὸ πῦρ.

Gloss: "I call this dish the *rhodônia* (Rose-garden). It is made so that you get your garland-seasoning not only on your head but inside yourself… Having pounded the most fragrant roses in a mortar (*igdis*), I put in the brains of birds and pigs, well boiled and thoroughly stripped of sinew, and the golden parts of the eggs (yolks), and after them oil, *garon* (fish sauce), pepper, wine. Having ground all this carefully, I put it into a new shallow dish (*lopas*), giving a gentle and continuous fire."

Note: the dish-name is **ῥοδωνιά** (*rhodônia*, "rose-garden"), Yonge's "Dish of Roses" — cf. Apicius' title *patina de rosis / patinam decoris*.

### Word-for-word alignment against Apicius 4.2.9

Apicius Latin (Grocock–Grainger 4.2.9 = *patinam decoris*):
> patinam decoris: accipies rosas et exfoliabis; album tolles; mittes in mortarium, suffundes liquamen, fricabis, postea mittes liquaminis ciatum I semis et sucum per colum colabis. accipies cerebella quattuor, eneruabis et teres piperis scripulos VIII; suffundes ex suco, fricabis, postea oua VIII frangis, uini ciatum unum semis et passi ciatum I, olei modicum. postea patinam perungues et eam in termospodio piperis puluerem super asparges et inferes.

| Greek (Ath. 9.406) | Latin (Apic. 4.2.9) | Gloss / note |
|---|---|---|
| ῥόδα τὰ εὐοσμότατα | rosas et exfoliabis; album tolles | Roses. **Latin adds** two prep steps: strip petals, remove the white heel. Greek only says "most fragrant". |
| ἐν ἴγδει τρίψας | mittes in mortarium … fricabis | Pound in a mortar. **Close match.** |
| γάρον | suffundes liquamen … liquaminis ciatum I semis; sucum per colum colabis | Fish sauce (*garon*/*liquamen*). **Latin adds** a measured 1½ cyathi + straining the rose-liquamen through a sieve. |
| ἐγκεφάλους ὀρνίθων τε καὶ χοίρων … ἐξινιασθέντας (ἑφθούς) | cerebella quattuor, eneruabis | Brains, de-sinewed. **Close match.** Greek specifies **birds + pigs** and **boiled**; Latin gives a **quantity (4)** but leaves species/boiling unstated. |
| πέπερι | teres piperis scripulos VIII | Pepper. **Latin quantifies** (8 scruples). |
| τῶν ᾠῶν τὰ χρυσᾶ | oua VIII frangis | Eggs. Greek specifies **yolks only** ("the golden parts"); Latin uses **8 whole eggs**. |
| οἶνον | uini ciatum unum semis | Wine. **Latin quantifies** (1½ cyathi). |
| — (no counterpart) | et passi ciatum I | **Latin adds** 1 cyathus *passum* (raisin-wine). |
| ἔλαιον | olei modicum | Oil ("a little"). **Match.** |
| εἰς λοπάδα καινήν … ἁπαλὸν καὶ συνεχὲς διδοὺς τὸ πῦρ | patinam perungues … in termospodio | New shallow dish + gentle steady fire ≈ greased *patina* baked in *thermospodium* (hot ashes/embers). **Close match** (Greek "new" dish vs Latin "greased" pan). |
| — (Greek ends on the perfume) | piperis puluerem super asparges et inferes | **Latin adds** a pepper-dust garnish and the serving instruction. |

### Verdict: close cousins / shared culinary tradition — NOT a literal translation

Same dish, ingredient-for-ingredient: roses pounded in a mortar, de-sinewed brains, egg (yolk), oil, fish sauce, pepper, wine, cooked gently in a shallow dish. This is unmistakably one recipe, not a coincidental overlap. But it is **not** a word-for-word translation of this Greek text:

- **Grammatical frame differs.** Greek is a first-person aorist cook's *boast* ("ἐπέβαλον… ἐνέβαλον" — I put in… I put in); Latin is second-person future *instructions* ("accipies… mittes" — you will take… you will put).
- **Latin is the fuller recipe.** It adds precise metrology (4 brains, 8 scruples pepper, 8 eggs, cyathus measures), an extra ingredient (*passum*), a straining step, a greasing step, and a pepper garnish + serving line.
- **Greek is more specific on two points** the Latin leaves vague: brains are **avian + porcine and boiled**, and only the **yolks** are used.

Best characterisation: **shared-archetype cousins** — two independent renderings (one a comic-flavoured monologue, one a working recipe) of the same dish circulating in Greco-Roman elite cookery around the 1st–3rd c AD. The Latin is not a translation of this Greek passage, and neither is demonstrably prior. This is a genuinely strong parallel worth citing on the Apicius page; frame it as "a near-identical Greek version, the *rhodônia*, appears in Athenaeus 9.406", **not** as a source that predates Apicius.

### Scholarship

- The recipe is edited and translated in **[Grocock & Grainger (2006)](https://books.google.com/books/about/Apicius.html?id=5nVfAAAAMAAJ)**, *Apicius: A Critical Edition* (Prospect Books), as 4.2.9 *patinam decoris*.
- A quick search did not surface a secondary source that explicitly pairs Apicius 4.2.9 with Athenaeus 9.406 in print; popular write-ups of *patina de rosis* (e.g. Pass the Flamingo, Atlas Obscura) discuss the Apician recipe without noting the Greek parallel. The parallel is drawn here directly from the two primary texts. Worth a deeper library check (Grocock–Grainger commentary, Dalby's *Siren Feasts* / *Food in the Ancient World*) before asserting "scholars connect these" on the public page — treat the connection as this project's own observation for now.
