# Vardanus in Apicius 6.8.11 — who or what the name refers to

*Created 2026-07-11 14:24.*

## TL;DR

**"Vardanus" is not attested as an ordinary personal name anywhere I could find in the Roman world** — every attestation of Vardanes/Vardanus/Οὐαρδάνης that surfaces is one of the two Parthian kings; no slave, freedman, or provincial of that name turned up in any general or targeted search. But that negative rests on general web search, not a clean hit inside EDCS/PIR/Trismegistos, which all blocked automated access — treat it as strong-suggestive, not proven. **Apicius commentators do not identify a "Vardanus" at all**: Grocock & Grainger (2006) footnote the reader to their Appendix 3 for the person the dish is named for, but Appendix 3 contains no such entry (a genuine loose end in the edition). Older editors (Vehling, following Humelbergius) treated `Vardanus` as corrupt and emended toward **`Varianus`/`Varus`** — i.e. Quintilius Varus, read as a gluttony reference — and the manuscripts themselves disagree (`Vardanus` / `Vardamus`). **My bottom line: corruption/uncertain-reading ~45%, named for the Parthian king Vardanes ~35%, named for a non-royal individual ~20%.** The Parthian-king reading is genuinely plausible and, as far as I can tell, unpublished — but it competes with a real textual-corruption problem that the standard editions take seriously.

Everything below flags what is sourced vs. my own inference. I could not directly query EDCS, PIR, or Trismegistos (all returned 403/redirects to JS front-ends); the prosopographical "nothing found" is therefore an argument from the failure of many searches, not from a database run.

---

## 1. Parthian kings named Vardanes

**Vardanes I** (r. c. AD 40–47; sources vary, some give a co-rule from the late 30s). Son of Artabanus II/III, in a long civil war with his brother Gotarzes II; assassinated while hunting. He is **the Parthian king a Roman elite audience of the 1st c. AD would plausibly have known** ([Livius, "Vardanes I"](https://www.livius.org/articles/person/vardanes-i/); [Wikipedia, "Vardanes I of Parthia"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vardanes_I_of_Parthia)).
- **Tacitus, *Annals* 11.8–10** narrates his reign, the war with Gotarzes, the revolt of Seleucia, and his ambitions toward Armenia that risked war with Rome (Livius, above; the same passages surfaced across searches).
- **Josephus, *Antiquities* 20.69–74** mentions his succession and Armenian designs (Livius, above).
- **Prominence (my assessment):** moderate-to-good. Vardanes I is a named antagonist in Tacitus at exactly the period the *De re coquinaria* material coalesced. A cultured Roman reading Tacitus/senatorial gossip would recognize "Vardanes" as a recent Parthian king. This is the strongest structural support for the king-eponym hypothesis: the name would *register* as royal-Parthian to the intended audience.

**Vardanes II** (r. c. AD 55–58), a rebel/pretender against his father Vologases I in Media/Hyrcania; possibly Roman-backed; documented mainly in **Tacitus, *Annals*** ([Livius, "Vardanes II"](https://www.livius.org/articles/person/vardanes-ii/); [Wikipedia, "Vardanes II of Parthia"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vardanes_II_of_Parthia)). Lower profile than Vardanes I.

**Inference:** if the dish is royal-Parthian, Vardanes I is the far likelier referent than Vardanes II. Note the *dish itself is not Eastern* — a pale milk-and-egg-white *ius candidum*, pepper the only spice (confirmed by the recipe text and by [Grainger's *Cooking Apicius*](https://prospectbooks.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/Cooking_Apicius-extract.pdf) description: "no spices apart from pepper... subtle... creamy"). So a Parthian eponym would be an honorific/naming flourish, *not* an ethnic-cuisine label — unlike the adjacent `pullus Parthicus` (6.8.3/6.9.13), which really is styled Eastern (asafoetida, etc.). That contrast cuts both ways: it makes "Vardanus = Parthian ethnicity" unlikely (that role is already filled by `Parthicus`), which is *consistent* with Vardanus being a personal name (royal or not) rather than a toponym.

## 2. Prosopography — is Vardanes/Vardanus an ordinary Roman personal name?

**No hit found for any non-royal Vardanus/Vardanes/Οὐαρδάνης** in the Roman world across repeated general and targeted searches (CIL/EDCS, PIR, Trismegistos, LGPN, slave-name studies). Every retrieved attestation was a Parthian king or the Apicius dish. Caveats on method:
- **EDCS** (Clauss–Slaby, ~500k Latin inscriptions): its search now redirects to a JS front-end at `edcs.hist.uzh.ch` that I could not query programmatically. No inscription surfaced via web search either.
- **PIR** ([pir.bbaw.de](https://pir.bbaw.de/)) and **Trismegistos** ([trismegistos.org](https://www.trismegistos.org/)) both returned 403 to automated fetches; no Vardanes entry surfaced via search.
- The one adjacent real-name datum: the later **Byzantine** usurper Bardanes/Vardanes (Philippicus, r. 711–713) — irrelevant chronologically, but it confirms the form persisted as an Iranian-origin personal name.

**Significance (inference):** The *absence* is itself meaningful because the editors who could query the corpora (see §4) did **not** identify a real Vardanus and instead reached for emendation. If a freedman/cook named Vardanes were on record, that "named for its maker" reading would be the natural solution and someone would likely have proposed it. Nobody has. So the non-royal-individual hypothesis is *possible* (Iranian-named slaves/freedmen certainly existed in Rome) but currently **unsupported by any concrete attestation I can point to.** I did not achieve a definitive database run, so I cannot exclude a stray epigraphic Vardanes.

## 3. Etymology / origin of the name

Iranian, from Middle Persian/Parthian **Wardān**, generally connected to *ward*/*vard* "**rose**" (the same root behind Armenian *vard* "rose"); an Iranian name, borne by royalty (the Parthian kings) but not *exclusively* royal — it is well attested later as an ordinary Armenian name (e.g. Vardan Mamikonian, 5th c.), and variants Vartan/Vardan/Bardanes recur ([Behind the Name, "Vardan"](https://www.behindthename.com/name/vardan); corroborated by general onomastic sources). So etymologically it is a normal Iranian personal name, not an inherently royal title. **Inference:** this weakly supports "could be a non-royal individual" — the name *type* is compatible with a slave/freedman of Eastern origin — but doesn't evidence one.

## 4. Apicius scholarship on 6.8.11

- **Grocock & Grainger (2006), *Apicius: A Critical Edition*** (Prospect Books): translate it "Chicken or guinea-fowl in the style of Vardanus" and **footnote the reader to Appendix 3 for the possible person the dish is named for — but Appendix 3 has no Vardanus entry.** This is documented by a cook working directly from the edition ([Edesian Experiments blog, Oct 2025](http://edesianexperiments.blogspot.com/), quoting: the footnote "tells the reader to refer to Appendix 3... there is no mention of any 'Vardanus' in Appendix 3 (oops)"; corroborated via the [ckbk edition of *Cooking Apicius*](https://app.ckbk.com/recipe/cook18446c02s001r009/chicken-or-guinea-fowl-in-the-style-of-vardanus)). Net: **the standard modern critical edition does not actually say who Vardanus is.** I was not able to read the printed Appendix 3 directly to confirm no relevant name hides elsewhere in it.
- **Vehling (1936)** and the older tradition (via **Humelbergius**), as preserved in the **LacusCurtius** apparatus to Book VI: heading given as "Chicken à la Varus / Pullus Vardanus," with manuscript/editorial variants **`Vardanus` (G.–V.)**, **`Vardamus` (Tor.)**, and **`Varianus`** (Humelbergius's emendation). Humelbergius suggested the dish honored "emperor Varianus (?)" or, alternatively, **Quintilius Varus**, read as a byword for gluttony ([LacusCurtius, Apicius Book VI](https://penelope.uchicago.edu/Thayer/E/Roman/Texts/Apicius/6*.html)). So the older scholarship treats the word as **corrupt and probably = Varianus/Varus**, not as a securely transmitted "Vardanus."
- **Jacques André, *Apicius: L'art culinaire* (Les Belles Lettres, 1974)**: I could not retrieve his note on 6.8.11; the edition is not online. Flag as a gap — André's apparatus is the place most likely to state the manuscript reading and any onomastic view definitively, and it should be checked in print.
- **Milham Teubner (1969)**: not retrieved online; another print gap.

**Key scholarly takeaway:** no published source I found endorses the Parthian-king identification. The live scholarly options in print are (a) leave it as the transmitted `Vardanus` (unexplained), or (b) emend to `Varianus`/read as `Varus`. The Vardanes-king reading appears genuinely absent from the literature — consistent with the task's premise.

## 5. Other ancient "Vardanus" dishes/preparations

None found. The only culinary attestation is this single Apicius entry; there is no independent ancient reference to a Vardanus-named sauce or preparation. (There *is* a river Vardanes/Vardanus — an ancient name associated with a river in the Kuban/Sindica region near the Black Sea — but nothing links it to food; noted only for completeness, low relevance.)

## Bottom line — king vs. individual vs. corruption

The three hypotheses and my rough split:

| Hypothesis | Probability | Main support | Main problem |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Textual corruption / uncertain reading** (→ *Varianus*, *Varus*, or just unstable) | **~45%** | MSS disagree (`Vardanus`/`Vardamus`); standard editors emend; Apicius names are notoriously corrupt | Requires the transmitted form to be wrong; `Vardanus` *is* the majority reading |
| **Named for the Parthian king Vardanes (I)** | **~35%** | Name registers as royal-Parthian to a 1st-c. audience (Tacitus/Josephus); other Apicius eponyms are personal names; dish being non-Eastern fits an honorific rather than an ethnic label | No ancient or modern source asserts it; would be a unique *foreign-royal* eponym in a corpus whose personal eponyms are otherwise Roman insiders |
| **Named for a non-royal individual** (Eastern-named slave/freedman/cook) | **~20%** | Name-type is a normal Iranian personal name compatible with servile/freedman onomastics; "named for its maker" is a common pattern | **Zero attested Vardanus/Vardanes** as an ordinary person found; if one existed, editors would likely have flagged it |

**My reading (inference, flagged as such):** the honest state of play is that Apicius's `Vardanus` is a crux. The *most defensible published* position is that the reading is unstable and the older editors were probably right to worry about it. But the Parthian-king hypothesis is not a stretch — Vardanes I was a real, Tacitus-attested name in exactly the right period, the dish's non-Eastern character actually *fits* an honorific-personal eponym better than an ethnic one, and the reason nobody has published it may simply be that no one connected the Apicius crux to the Arsacid king-list. It deserves a footnote as a genuine possibility, hedged: the competing corruption problem is real, and there is no positive evidence tying the dish to any specific Vardanes. Do **not** state the king identification as fact on the site.
