# Book recommendations

Last updated June 2026  

## Science

- _Power, Sex, Suicide_ by Nick Lane (2005)

  about mitochondria, obviously

- _Revolutions that made the Earth_ by Tim Lenton and Andrew Watson (2011)

  about how life has radically changed the climate and geochemistry of our planet

- _The Logic of Chance_ by Eugene Koonin (2011)

  on evolution

- _The Secret Life of Science_ by Jeremy Baumberg (2018)

  on how science actually works

- _Alien Oceans_ by Kevin Hand (2020)

  on why we should look for aliens on Enceladus (amongst other places)

- _Science Fictions_ (2020) and _Intelligence, all that matters_ (2015) by Stuart Ritchie

  the first on the replication crisis, the second is a good concise introduction to research on intelligence

- _The Worst of Times_ by Paul Wignall (2015)

  on the end-Permian and end-Triassic mass extinctions

- _The Discovery of Global Warming_ by Spencer Weart (2003)

- _Exercised_ by Daniel Lieberman (2020)

  on exercise and health

- _Mom Genes_ by Abigail Tucker (2021)

  on the biology of motherhood

- _Viruses: Intimate Invaders_, by Van Wilson (2022)

- _T: The Story of Testosterone_, by Carole Hooven (2021)

- _Immune_, by Philipp Dettmer (2021)

  a surprisingly detailed pop-sci discussion about how the immune system works

- _Nose Dive_, by Harold McGee (2020)

  on smells, fair and foul, and the chemicals underlying them

- _A City on Mars_ by Zach and Kelly Weinersmith (2023)

  about why space colonization is unrealistic and pointless

- _The Origins of Genomic Architecture_ by Michael Lynch (2007)

  not aimed at lay readers but a fascinating nevertheless for those with some background in biology

- _Understanding Bird Behavior_ by Wenfei Tong (2020)

  a very cute book

- _The Emperor of All Maladies_ by Siddhartha Mukherjee (2010)

  a history of cancer treatment and research

- _The Simple Science of Flight_ by Henk Tennekes (1996)

## Engineering and Technology

- _Structures, or why things don't fall down_, by JE Gordon (1978)

- _Genentech: The Beginnings of Biotech_ by Sally Hughes (2011)

- _How Big Things Get Done_ by Bent Flyvbjerg (2023)

  about megaprojects and why they often go radically over-budget

- _Dealers of Lightning_ by Michael Hiltzik (1999)

  a history of PARC in the 1970s, part of Xerox, where many key computing innovations occurred

- _The Origins of Efficiency_ by Brian Potter (2025)

- _The Infinity Machine_ by Sebastian Mallaby (2026)

  about Demis Hassabis and DeepMind

## Philosophy

- _Other Minds_ by Peter Godfrey-Smith (2016)

  philosophical work on octopuses

- _Against Empathy_ by Paul Bloom (2016)

- _Deep Utopia_ by Nick Bostrom (2024)

  an odd, at times quite funny, at times quite moving, discussion of what to do once everything is solved

## Psychology

- _The Mating Mind_ by Geoffrey Miller (2000)

  on the role of sexual selection in human evolution

- _The Elephant in the Brain_ by Kevin Simler and Robin Hanson (2018)

- _Why Women Have Sex_ by Meston and Buss (2009)

  pretty self-explanatory

- _Never Enough_ by Judith Grisel (2019)

  on drugs

- _Good Reasons for Bad Feelings_ by Randolph Nesse (2019)

  on the evolutionary origin of mental illness

- _The State of Affairs_ by Esther Perel (2017)

  on affairs

- _Algorithms to Live By_ by Brian Christian and Tom Griffiths (2016)

- _Stumbling on Happiness_ by Daniel Gilbert (2006)

  on how to be happy

- _Peak: Secrets from the New Science of Expertise_ (2016)

  much better than I was expecting given the title (the secret is lots of dedicated purposeful practice, if you are wondering)

## Sociology

- _Very Important People_ by Ashley Mears (2020)

  on the global partying scene

- _Addiction by Design_ by Natasha Schüll (2012)

  on slot machines, and the people who use them

- _Moral Mazes_ by Robert Jackall (1988)

  a sociologist describes middle and upper management in corporations he studied, and the petty politicking and gross incompetence that the management structure encourages

- _Everybody Lies_ by Seth Stephens-Davidowitz (2017)

  on using Google search data to investigate what people actually think about things

- _Codes of the Underworld: How Criminals Communicate_ by Diego Gambetta (2009)

  applies signalling theory to criminal enterprises

- _Status and Culture_ (2022), and _Blank Space_ (2025), by W. David Marx

## Anthropology

- _The Secret of Our Success_ (2015) and _The WEIRDest people in the world_ (2020) by Joseph Henrich

- _Religion Explained_ by Pascal Boyer (2001)

- _Gods of the Upper Air_ by Charles King (2019)

  On pioneering anthropologist Boas and his students

## Political Science

- _The Dictator's Handbook_ by de Mesquita and Smith (2011)

  full of useful advice

- _Coup d'Etat: A Practical Handbook_ by Edward Luttwak (1968)

  also full of useful advice

- _Seizing Power_ by Naunihal Singh (2014)

  the best book on coups I am aware of

- _The Origins of Political Order_ (2011) and _Political Order and Political Decay_ (2014) by Francis Fukuyama

- _Only the Dead_, by Bear Braumoeller (2019)

  making the case that we don't have evidence for a decline in war over the past few centuries

- _Why We Fight_ by Christopher Blattman (2022)

  on the causes of war

- _Destined For War_ by Graham Allison (2017)

  about the possibility of a US-China, and how to mitigate the risks

## Economics

### General

- _The Company of Strangers: A Natural History of Economic Life_ (2004) and _The Divine Economy_ (2024) by Paul Seabright

  the latter is a look at modern religion through the lens of economics

- _The Invisible Hook_ by Peter Leeson (2009)

  on the economics of piracy

- _Poor Economics_ by Banerjee and Duflo (2011)

- Anything by Bryan Caplan

  in particular _The Case Against Education_ (2018) and _The Myth of the Rational Voter_ (2007)

- _More Money Than God_ by Sebastian Mallaby (2010)

  a history of hedge funds, surprisingly fun

- _Lying for Money_ by Daniel Davies (2018)

  a natural history of fraud

- _GDP: a brief but affectionate history_ by Diane Coyle (2014)

- _The Box_ by Marc Levinson (2006)

  a surprisingly interesting book on shipping containers

### Economic History

*Arranged roughly by how technical/dry they are.*

- _How the World became Rich_ by Mark Koyama and Jared Rubin (2022)

  probably the best place to start

- _Global Economic History: A Very Short Introduction_ by Robert Allen (2011)

- _The Rise and Fall of American Growth_ by Robert Gordon (2016)

- _Power and Plenty: Trade, War, and the World Economy in the Second Millennium_ by Findlay and O'Rourke (2007)

- _Bourgeois Dignity_ by Deirdre McCloskey (2010)

  on why every explanation for the great divergence except Deirdre McCloskey's is bad

- _A Culture of Growth_ by Joel Mokyr (2016)

  a cultural explanation for the great divergence

- _Rulers, Religion, and Riches_ by Jared Rubin (2017)

- _The Long Divergence_ by Timur Kuran (2010)

  why Islamic law held back commerce in the Islamic world

- _Trade and Poverty_ by Jeffery Williamson (2011)

  why the 3rd world fell behind

- _Coercion, Capital, and European States_ by Charles Tilly (1990)

- _The Roman Market Economy_ by Peter Temin (2012)

  textbook on the economy of Rome

- _The Economic History of China_ by Richard von Glahn (2016)

  best (only?) work on this subject, be warned it is a bit dry

## History

### General

- _War in Human Civilization_ by Azar Gat (2006)

  best book on war in existence

- _Pre-Industrial Societies: Anatomy of the Pre-Modern World_ by Patricia Crone (1989)

  highly recommended for an overview of how, broadly speaking, pre-industrial societies actually operated

- _Escape from Rome: The Failure of Empire and the Road to Prosperity_ by Walter Scheidel (2019)

  about why the Roman empire formed, why no later power managed to recreate the empire, and how this important for understanding why Europe industrialized first

- _Why The West Rules for Now_ by Ian Morris (2010)

  long, but serves as a good introduction to both Western and Chinese history, in parallel.

- _Foragers, Farmers, and Fossil Fuels_, also by Ian Morris (2015)

  on how the values we hold have changed over time

- _The Big Book of Horrible Things_ by Matthew White (2011)

  a fascinating (if somewhat arbitrary) discussion of the 100 worst atrocities in history, ranked by number of deaths. The author also has an (appropriately dark) sense of humour.

- _After Tamerlane_ by John Darwin (2007)

  a history of European colonialism and the evolution of the great power system over the past five hundred years

- _Plagues Upon the Earth_ by Kyle Harper (2021)

  a history of diseases and pandemics

- _Famine, a short history_ by Cormac O Grada (2009)

### Regional Histories

- _A History of China_ (2008) and _A History of India_ (2000) by John Keay

  probably the best single volume introductions to these topics in English, and both are very readable

- _A Brief History of Mexico_ by Lynn Foster (1997)

- _The Greeks: A Global History_ by Roderick Beaton (2021)

### Ancient/Medieval

- _Who we are and how we got here_ by David Reich (2018)

  on how recent genetic evidence has given us a much better understanding of human prehistory

- _Against the Grain_ by James Scott (2017)

  or agriculture "it's a trap!"

- _The Indo-Europeans Rediscovered_ by J. P. Mallory (2025)

- _Weavers, Scribes, and Kings_ by Amanda Podany (2022)

  a history of ancient Mesopotamia

- _Understanding Early Civilization_ by Bruce Trigger (2003)

  a comparative study of seven ancient societies

- _Ancient Israel: What we know and how we know it_ by Lester Grabbe (2007)

- _The Rise and Fall of Classical Greece_ by Josiah Ober (2015)

  poli-sci influenced look at the subject

- _The Fate of Rome_ by Kyle Harper (2017)

  a look at the role of climate, disease, and economic changes and their effect on the collapse of the Roman Empire

- _Surviving Rome_ by Kim Bowes (2025)

  more academic-oriented work, nevertheless gives a great overview of what we actually know about life for typical people living under Roman rule

- Anything by John Julius Norwich

  in particular his trilogy on Byzantine history, and his history of the papacy

- _Fifth Sun_ by Camilla Townsend (2019)

  a history of the Aztecs

- _The Four Heavens_ by David Stuart (2026)

  the current definitive history of the Mayans

- _The Carthaginians_ by Dexter Hoyos (2010)

  the definitive book on the subject

- _The Fires of Lust: Sex in the Middle Ages_ by Katherine Harvey (2021)

### Early Modern

- _1491_ (2005) and _1493_ (2011) by Charles Mann

  on the pre-Columbian Americas, and the Columbian exchange, respectively

- _Why Did Europe Conquer the World?_ by Phillip Hoffman (2015)

  econ history look at the question, very readable and short

- _The Gunpowder Age: China, Military Innovation, and the Rise of the West in World History_ by Tonio Andrade (2016)

  a look at how gunpowder weapons developed, good complement to the previous book

- _Osman's Dream_ by Caroline Finkel (2005)

  a history of the Ottoman empire

- _The Origins of Sex_ by Faramerz Dabhoiwala (2012)

- _The Anarchy: The Relentless Rise of the East India Company_ by William Dalrymple (2019)

- _Return of a King_ by William Dalrymple (2013)

  about the disastrous, comitragic First Anglo-Afghan War

### Modern History

- _Dancing in the Glory of Monsters_ (2011) and _The War That Doesn't Say Its Name_ (2021) by Jason Stearns

  the former book is a history of the Congo Wars, the deadliest wars that have occurred since the end of the second world war, and the latter is on the aftermath. Be warned that they are pretty dark in places, but very written and fascinating.

- _Cold War_ by John Gaddis (2005)

- _Command and Control_ by Eric Schlosser (2013)

  on early American nuclear weapon usage, and all the many many fuck ups that occurred

- _The Last Emperor of Mexico_ by Edward Shawcross (2021)

  the bizarre story of the short-lived second Mexican empire and its Austrian Habsburg ruler

- _Dictatorland_ by Paul Kenyon (2018)

  a description of six African dictators, all fascinating and disturbing—Macías Nguema is both the most obscure and most bizarre

- _Raven Rock_ by Garrett Graff (2017)

  a history of US continuity of government planning in the face of nuclear war

- _The Wages of Destruction_ by Adam Tooze (2006)

  an economic history of Nazi Germany

- _A Short History of World War 1_ by James Stokesbury (1981)

- _The Making of the Atomic Bomb_ by Richard Rhodes (1986)

- _Red Famine_ by Anne Applebaum (2017)

  a history of the Ukrainian famine in the 1930s

- _The Doomsday Machine_ by Daniel Ellsberg (2017)

- _The Dead Hand_ by David Hoffman (2009)

- _Restricted Data_ by Alex Wellerstein (2021)

  a history of nuclear secrecy

- _The Napoleonic Wars: A Global History_ by Alexander Mikaberidze (2020)

- _Freedom's Forge_ by Arthur Herman (2012)

- _Modern Paraguay_ by Tomas Mandl (2021)

  not a narrative history, more a portrait of the modern country

- _King Leopold's Ghost_ by Adam Hochschild (1998)

  on the Congo Free State under King Leopold II

- _The Soviet Bioweapons Program_ by Leitenberg and Zilinskas (2012)

  the definitive work on the topic, surprisingly readable

- _How to Hide an Empire_ by Daniel Immerwahr (2019)

  a history of the "Greater USA", particular the Philippines and Puerto Rico

- _The Making of the Modern Philippines_ by Philip Bowring (2022)

- _Another Day of Life_ (1976) and _The Emperor_ (1978) by Ryszard Kapuscinski

  the former on the Angolan civil war, the latter an unconventional and not wholly-reliable portrait of Haile Selassie

- _Embers of War_ by Frederik Logevall (2012)

  about the French Indochina war, and how the US first came to be entangled with Vietnam

### Other History

- _A History of the Bible_ by John Barton (2019)

  more interesting than it sounds

- _The Allure of Battle_ by Cathal Nolan (2017)

  or, why you shouldn't spend your time trying to win wars by seeking big battles AKA don't be Germany in WW1 or WW2

- _Christian Beginnings_ by Geza Vermes (2012)

  covers the first ~300 years of the Christian church

- _Buddhism_ (2001) and _The Buddha_ (2025), both by Donald Lopez

  scholarly and concise

- _Religions of Early India: A Cultural History_ by Richard Davis (2024)

## Biography

- Everything by Robert Caro

- _The Fish that Ate the Whale_ by Rich Cohen (2012)

  biography of Sam Zemurray, "the banana man" who became president of the United Fruit Company

- _Nine Lives_ by William Dalrymple (2009)

  a moving work exploring the lives of nine unusual religious figures in modern South Asia

- _Nine Lives_ by Aimen Dean (2018)

  the story of an al-Qaeda member who became a British double agent

## Other

- _The Sense of Style_ by Steven Pinker (2014)

  on how to write good

- _Resetting the Table_ by Robert Paarlberg (2021)

  on various issues related to modern food production and consumption

- _Good work if you can get it_ by Jason Brennan (2020)

  on modern academia

- _Factfulness_ by Hans Rosling (2018)

- _The Power of Babel_ by John McWhorter (2001)

  on linguistics

- _Pronoun Trouble_ by John McWhorter (2025)

  short, fascinating look at how English got its pronouns

- _Order without Design_ by Alain Bertaud (2018)

  on urban planning

- _The Book_ by Keith Houston (2016)

  on the history of the book as a physical object

- Anything by A J Jacobs

  if you are looking for something lighter and funnier — maybe try _Drop Dead Healthy_ (2012) or _The Year of Living Biblically_ (2007) to start

- _How not to write a novel_ by Mittelmark and Newman (2008)

  an amusing book which explains how not to write a novel by giving many terrible examples of what not to do
